Framework · Implementation & Customer Success

The Adoption Gap

Complex B2B software gets bought, gets configured… and then a meaningful share of users quietly drift back to their spreadsheets. This is a reusable framework for closing the gap between a customer buying a platform and a customer actually using it.

Adoption is a behavior-change problem wearing a software costume. Configuring the platform is the easy part. The hard part is earning the trust of someone who was already good at the old way.

The whole framework in one picture. Effort comes before payoff. During that trough, the path of least resistance is the old spreadsheet — so the job is to get to the first real win before the team relapses.

How I work

Three operating principles

01

Listen first

The client bought this for a reason. The job isn't to push features — it's to show them that reason in play.

02

Push back honestly

No false hopes, no dismissing concerns. Both break trust, and adoption runs entirely on trust.

03

Value before features

Get them to the win that made them buy, fast. The feature tour can wait until they care.

Part 1 · The people

Map who resists, and why

You can't drive adoption across a team by treating "the customer" as one entity. A complex platform touches several roles at once, and each weighs the switch differently. The job is to know who's in the trough and what's keeping them there.

PersonaWhat they fearFailure mode if ignoredThe move
Executive sponsorWasted spendDisengages, no air-coverMilestone updates tied to ROI

Adoption happens when the skeptical practitioner stops keeping a shadow spreadsheet and the occasional user stops emailing requests. Those two conversions are the real finish line.

Part 2 · Implementation

Onboarding, organized around the first win

Days 0–30

Land one win

Pick one high-pain, self-contained workflow. Configure only what it needs. Train the champion and the skeptic on their real data. Run it end-to-end once.

Success: one real workflow has run in the platform, and the sponsor has a win to report.
Days 31–60

Build the habit

Bring the rest of the team onto the proven workflow. Target the skeptic directly. Watch for the shadow spreadsheet. Run the health score for the first time.

Success: the workflow is the default, and the skeptic has stopped maintaining a shadow copy.
Days 61–90

Widen & prove ROI

Add a second workflow now that trust exists. Bring the sponsor a results story tied to the original business case. Transition to steady-state success.

Success: two workflows live, value demonstrated, a clear path to renewal.
Part 3 · Customer Success

Detect who's sliding back — early

"They went live" is not "they adopted." A health score is only useful if it tracks the behaviors that actually predict retention. The deepest signal is simple: has the work genuinely moved into the platform, or is it still happening somewhere else?

What's weighted: workflow migration depth (30%) · breadth of active users (25%) · engagement depth (20%) · key-feature adoption (15%) · relationship & sentiment (10%). Logins are a vanity metric — every signal here is chosen to detect reversion to the old way.

Healthy

Across the trough. Workflow migrated, multiple users active, key features in use.
Play: protect and grow. Reinforce ROI with the sponsor; look for expansion.

At risk

Sitting in the trough, could go either way. Narrow adoption or slipping engagement.
Play: intervene now. Diagnose the weak signal and target it. This is where good CS earns its keep.

Critical

Sliding back to the old way — and may not realize it's churning.
Play: escalate. Re-establish the "why," and re-run a compressed onboarding around one new win.

A health score with no playbook attached is just a dashboard. Each tier maps to a default action, and each weak signal maps to a specific fix.
